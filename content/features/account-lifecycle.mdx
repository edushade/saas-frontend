faq:
  title: "Account Lifecycle FAQ"
  description: "The questions that come up when you're setting up sign-up, deactivation and offboarding for a real platform."
  items:
    - question: "What states can an account be in?"
      answer: "Five — pending verification, active, inactive, suspended and deleted. Pending is the starting line. Active is the operating state. Inactive is a calm pause. Suspended is a stricter pause, usually with a reason on the record. Deleted is a soft delete that keeps the data safe for recovery."
    - question: "How does a new account become active?"
      answer: "It verifies. New users get an email link or an OTP — whichever they signed up with — and once they confirm, the platform flips them to active and lets them in. If they don't verify in time, they stay in pending and can request a fresh code."
    - question: "What happens if someone never verifies?"
      answer: "They stay in pending verification with no access. The verification carries an expiry and a max attempts setting, so a stale OTP won't keep working forever. If they come back later, they can request a fresh one — no need to start the sign-up over."
    - question: "Can users deactivate themselves?"
      answer: "Yes. From their profile under Account → Deletion, a learner can start the process themselves. The platform confirms what they're doing, holds the request, and the deactivation goes through with everything they've earned safely preserved in case they return."
    - question: "What's the difference between inactive and suspended?"
      answer: "Inactive is a friendly pause — usually triggered by the user themselves, or by an admin when someone is on a break. Suspended is a stricter state, usually because of a policy matter, with a reason on the record. Both stop access. Both can be reversed. The difference is mostly intent and tone."
    - question: "What happens to a learner's progress when their account changes state?"
      answer: "Nothing — it's preserved. Inactive, suspended or even soft-deleted accounts still hold the courses they took, the quizzes they completed, the certificates they earned and the time they put in. If they come back, every bit of that history comes back with them."
    - question: "Is delete really delete?"
      answer: "It's a soft delete. The account stops working straight away — no logins, no notifications, no access — but the record is held in the background with a deleted_at timestamp. An admin can reactivate it later if the situation calls for that, and the user picks up where they left off."
    - question: "What does reactivation look like?"
      answer: "An admin opens the deleted user from the People view and chooses Reactivate. The platform clears the deletion, drops the account back into pending verification with a fresh start, and asks the user to verify their email or phone again. Once they do, they're active and back in."
    - question: "Do users get told when their state changes?"
      answer: "Yes. The notifications system has templates for every transition — deactivated, reactivated, suspended, deleted, restored. They go out by email, SMS, in-app and push, in the user's language. An account never quietly changes state without the person knowing."
    - question: "Is every change recorded?"
      answer: "Every one. Status changes, role changes, permission grants, impersonation start and stop — all of them land in the audit log automatically with the actor, the target and the time. You don't have to remember to log it. Open Admin → Audit and the history is right there."
    - question: "What if someone signs up again with the same email after deletion?"
      answer: "The platform checks the OAuth side too — if they sign in with the same Google or GitHub account they used before deletion, the system can offer to reactivate the existing record and link the new login to it. They keep their history; you keep one clean account per person."
    - question: "Can a tenant set its own deletion or retention policy?"
      answer: "Yes. Edushade is multi-tenant and each organization controls its own configuration — sign-up rules, verification requirements, reactivation policy, retention. The lifecycle states are the same everywhere, but how strictly you apply them is up to you."
    - question: "Are admins protected from being deleted accidentally?"
      answer: "Sensitive actions are gated by permission. Deleting another admin or staff member needs the right role, and the audit trail records who took the action with all the context. There's also no one-click hard delete on the user surface — every deletion goes through soft delete first, so accidents are recoverable."
    - question: "Does the audit log show why something changed, not just what?"
      answer: "Where the platform takes the change with a reason — like a suspension or a deletion that asks for a note — the reason lands on the record. For automatic changes (verification timeouts, OAuth-driven reactivation, expired session cleanup), the audit entry carries the trigger so the story is still readable months later."
